Jeremy Keith — Building (Full Stack Europe 2019) · Watch on YouTube

Getting to know the web

I went into Jeremy Keith’s talk expecting a typical update on tools and trends. Instead, he frames web development as something closer to architecture: not just assembling parts, but designing structures that should last, adapt, and remain useful over time.

That shift in perspective matters. The web isn’t only a polished interface or a bundle of JavaScript files—it’s an environment people depend on. Thinking like a builder means caring about fundamentals, clarity, and long-term impact.

Exploring parallels

One of the strongest ideas in the talk is the parallel between physical buildings and digital systems. In architecture, you plan for context, materials, maintenance, and change. On the web, this translates into standards, semantics, accessibility, and maintainability.

A site that cannot be understood or extended over time is like a temporary structure—impressive at first glance, but fragile in practice.

The current state

The talk feels like a quiet critique of a culture that prioritizes speed over durability. We optimize for shipping quickly, often forgetting that the web is a living space shaped by many devices, users, and conditions.

Building for the web means accepting uncertainty and designing for it, starting with strong foundations and adding complexity only when it truly serves a purpose.

Final thoughts

This talk left me with a simple but powerful mindset: treat web projects as real structures. Make them understandable, resilient, and open to change. You’re not just publishing a page—you’re building a place someone will enter.
